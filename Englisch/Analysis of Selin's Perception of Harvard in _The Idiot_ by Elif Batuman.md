In the extract from Elif Batumans novel "The Idiot" (2017) the author employs several narrative techniques to vividly show the reader how Selin perceives being a student au Harvard.

Through the first-person narration, readers dive into Selin's inner thoughts and emotions, experiencing her initial days at Harvard University. The repetitive phrasing of "long days and even longer nights" (ll. 1-2) lays the groundwork for the relentless endurance Selin faces, emphasizing her struggle and setting a melancholic tone from the outset. 
Moreover, the allusions to a "Renaissance person or a specialist" and the "Times Literary Supplement" underscore Selin's struggle to assimilate within the intellectual discourse at Harvard. These allusions heighten the reader's awareness of Selin's disorientation and alienation, vividly portraying her sense of having a hard time understanding the tricky or complicated ideas in her new surroundings.

The meticulous enumeration of Carol's background, encompassing her British accent, work at the Office of Information Technology, and degree in Old Norse, accentuates Selin's curiosity regarding Carol's role at the university. By highlighting Selin's perplexity, the enumeration invites readers to share in her intrigue, fostering curiosity and puzzlement about the enigmatic nature of Carol's position at Harvard.

Selin's use of hyperbole in feeling discouraged by repeated references to being a "little fish in a big sea" (l. 20) emphasizes her emotional distress and frustration within the academic setting. This hyperbolic expression intensifies the reader's empathy.

Furthermore, the metaphor of being a "little fish in a big sea" (l. 20) embodies Selin's perception of insignificance and being overwhelmed by the immense academic surroundings. This metaphor engenders profound sympathy within readers, allowing them to deeply connect with Selin's feelings of inadequacy and the challenges she faces in adapting to the university environment.

Selin's use of parallelism in detailing her efforts to apply to three literature seminars and being called in for only one interview accentuates her limited engagement within the academic spectrum. This parallel structure makes the reader's understand Selin's restricted participation, shedding light on the obstacles she encounters in accessing academic opportunities at Harvard.

Lastly, Selin's understatement during the interview, expressed as "I pretended to have to think it over 'No,' I said finally," (63-64) minimizes her discomfort and unease. This understated response intensifies the reader's empathy, hinting at the deeper distress Selin might have experienced, despite her attempt to downplay it.

Overall, Selin's portrayal in this passage suggests a profound disappointment rather than delight with her initiation at the university. Her feelings of misfit, discomfort, and uncertainty create a sense of unease within the reader, prompting reflections on the challenges of adaptation faced by individuals entering new academic environments. Through Selin's perspective, Batuman vividly captures the complexities of navigating the unfamiliar terrain of Harvard, leaving readers pondering the intricate facets of academic assimilation and personal identity within a collegiate setting.
