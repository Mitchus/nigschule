**1. The Current Situation in Germany**

On average, every German purchases 60 new items of clothing annually. Shockingly, only a small fraction of this ever-growing clothing mountain is worn regularly. This phenomenon, known as "Fast Fashion," involves the rapid production and consumption of fashion items, with trends changing at an increasingly rapid pace. High-street giants such as H&M and Zara change their collection up to 24 times yearly, prompting consumers to buy more frequently.

However, this comes with significant social and environmental costs. For instance, from a shirt priced at 29 euros in Germany, only 18 cents go to the workers involved in its production, according to the Clean Clothes Campaign. It is not surprising that about 40% of the clothes we purchase are thrown away or never worn, leading to a quicker disposal rate and shorter usage duration compared to 15 years ago. Germany alone sends a staggering one million tons of textiles to clothing collection points annually.

**2. Innovations and Combating Fast Fashion**
In response to the wastefulness of fast fashion, there has been a noticeable shift towards the sharing economy within the fashion sector. Here's how:

- **Homegrown Solutions:** Large brands are joining the sharing economy. Tchibo, a well-known Hamburg-based company, has introduced "Tchibo Share." This platform allows customers to rent instead of buy baby and children's clothing. The rental cost is affordable, with a jacket at four euros per month and a shirt at two euros. The emphasis is on sustainability, with at least 50% of the clothing made from organic cotton.

- **Established Players:** Another successful model is the Magdeburg-based online rental service Kilenda, operational since Autumn 2014. Kilenda offers approximately 15,000 items for children, pregnant women, and nursing mothers, along with toys. This service demonstrates that there are viable, eco-friendly alternatives to traditional retail.

- **Special Occasion Wear:** For items that are infrequently worn, such as expensive evening dresses, renting has become popular. Inspired by the American company Rent the Runway, similar platforms have emerged in Germany, such as Dresscoded and Chic by Choice. These services offer designer dresses and accessories for rental, starting at about 50 euros.

- **Everyday Fashion:** Foreveryday fashion, there's the Hamburg-based startup, Kleiderei. Since 2012, Kleiderei has offered a membership for 50 euros a month, providing a customized package of four clothing items each month. Members can return these items after a month or keep them longer. Innovatively, the founders are working on a feature that will allow users to rent out their own clothes.

**3. Grassroots Movements and Community Initiatives**

In addition to company-led efforts, grassroots movements are promoting sustainable fashion. Clothing swap parties, or "Kleidertausch," allow individuals to exchange garments they no longer need without any monetary transactions. Such events encourage the community spirit and reduce clothing waste. Notable events include Berlin's largest swap party "KleiderWandel," organized annually by Greenpeace and the Clean Clothes Campaign, attracting over 1,500 participants.

**Conclusion:**

The German landscape offers a progressive model in tackling fast fashion through innovative business models and community initiatives. These efforts not only bring practical solutions but also inspire a shift towards more sustainable consumer habits.

As members of an international group committed to changing consumer habits, we can draw valuable lessons from these examples. By supporting and promoting such initiatives globally, we can collectively make a significant impact on our planet’s well-being.

Creative AI: New Possibilities, Big Market, and Important Questions

Hello Readers!

Did you know that computers can now create art, music, and even stories? Recently, something amazing happened: a painting made by artificial intelligence (AI) was sold for over $400,000 at Christie’s, a famous auction house. This shows how AI is changing the world of creativity. Let’s explore the latest developments, the growing market, and the debates around this technology.

AI’s New Creative Skills
AI is now active in many creative fields. In art, the painting sold at Christie’s looked like a classical portrait, but it was made by a French group called Obvious using an AI program. However, this caused arguments: Is the real artist the AI, the programmers, or the people who used the program?

In music, big companies like Sony and Amazon are using AI. For example, Amazon’s Deep Music can change songs to match your mood. Other tools, like Amper Music, can create short music clips in just one minute. These clips are not perfect yet, but they are already used in ads or background music.

AI is also writing poems and song lyrics. Some programs can even make music that sounds like famous bands, such as the Beatles!

The Growing Market for Creative AI
The business around AI creativity is expanding quickly. Tech companies like Google and IBM are investing in AI projects. Music studios, like Abbey Road (where the Beatles recorded), are working with AI startups. Streaming services like Spotify use AI to create personalized playlists for users. The success of AI art sales, like the Christie’s auction, shows that this market has a lot of potential.

Critical Questions About AI Creativity
Despite the excitement, there are serious concerns. First, who owns AI-made art? If a computer creates a painting, should the credit go to the programmers, the users, or the AI itself? Laws about this are still unclear.

Second, critics say AI does not truly create new ideas. Instead, it mixes existing data. For example, the Christie’s painting used free online code, leading some to call it unoriginal.

Lastly, will AI replace human artists? Many experts believe AI is just a tool. It can help humans create, but it cannot copy the unique imagination of people.

Looking Ahead: Working With AI

While the technology offers exciting opportunities and a booming market, it also raises important questions about originality, ownership, and the role of humans in art.
