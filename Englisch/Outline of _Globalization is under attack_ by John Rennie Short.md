The article "Why there's a backlash against Globalization and what needs to change", written by John Rennie Short and published in "The Conversation," discusses the backlash against globalization through various political, economic, cultural developments.

It begins by highlighting political events such as Donald Trump's electoral victory, the Brexit vote, and the rise of nationalism in Europe and globally, reflecting growing dissatisfaction with globalization.
The article outlines the establishment of the global economic order post-WWII, with institutions like the IMF, World Bank, and WTO promoting open markets. Significant economic shifts, like Chinas entry into the World-market and Soviet collapse. These shifts led to wealth redistribution, with manufacturing moving to low-wage East Asia, causing a decline in Western working-class living standards and rising prosperity in China.
Globalization brought diversity but also fear and resentment towards immigrants and foreign cultures. 
Economic dislocation, with factory closures and job losses, particularly in the US, has empowered dissatisfaction and political instability, leading to a backlash against globalization.
Rennie also discusses the role of trade agreements like NAFTA and the expansion of the EU, facilitating globalization but facing resistance due to economic and social strains from EU enlargement.

Finally, the article talks about guiding globalization towards just and fair outcomes, addressing cultural connections, and promoting a connected, sustainable, and peaceful world. The importance of shaping globalization beyond the control of elites is underscored, ending with a call to focus on creating a more just and sustainable globalization project.
